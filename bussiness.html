About
iPrepare Business

Resilient businesses represent a crucial part of wider efforts to build safer communities and more secure societies. The private sector has been clearly acknowledged as a key partner and stakeholder in the post-2015 agenda for disaster risk reduction (DRR), including the Sendai Framework for DRR (SFDRR) 2015-2030.

Encouraging the adoption of risk informed business practices and risk sensitive investments are a key means of ensuring that economic and development gains are not undermined by damage and losses resulting from disaster events, including natural hazards. Efforts to engage the private sector in DRR initiatives should acknowledge the significant contribution of Small and Medium Sized Enterprises (SMEs) to national and regional economies, whilst recognizing the specific vulnerability and challenges faced by such businesses. This is particularly pertinent in the Asia Pacific region where SMEs account for a significant proportion of all business operations and employment.

The iPrepare Business facility at ADPC therefore aims to support the private sector to be an active stakeholder in Disaster Risk Reduction efforts in the Asia Pacific region by working in partnership with businesses, governments, policymakers and public organizations.

Business Continuity
Management

Disruptions to business can occur with or without warning. as seen in the 2011 floods in Thailand, the disaster severely affected some business operations more than one month; as well as, the cost of damage was rather high due to unplanned business continuity of the organization.

Effective business continuity planning establishes the mechanism for enterprises to maintain or quickly recover business operations when disruptive events, such floods and fire take place. It is expected that business continuity planning would help decrease the vulnerability of the market systems and help reduce the impact of disasters on the communities as well. Implementing BCM and preparing a Business Continuity Plan (BCP) can reduce the fear and alarm that are triggered by disruptions.

The planning process is as (or even more) important than the resulting plan. Insights gained from undergoing through the BCP preparation process are invaluable in helping business owners and their employees understand right down to the activities needed to keep the business running. BCM can uncover weaknesses in the business operations and can lead to the necessary corrective actions.

Before going to the next steps

We should understand terminologies related to business continuity management (BCM) and business continuity plan (BCP). Based on terminologies provided by ISO 22301: 2012 (Business Continuity Management Systems), definitions are as follows:

BCM

Holistic management process that identifies potential threats to an organization and the impacts to business operations those threats, if realized, might cause, and which provides a framework for building organizational resilience with the capability of an effective response that safeguards the interests of its key stakeholders, reputation, brand and value-creating activities.

BCP

Documentation procedures that guide organizations to respond, recover, resume, and restore to a pre-defined level of opera- tion following disruption.

5 Steps
for preparing a BCP

The APEC Small and Medium Enterprise Working Group (SMEWG) published the Guidebook on SME Business Continuity planning in 2013 However, we have simplified the steps in the Guidebook into 5 steps to make the preparation of the BCP easier for any enterprises

1. Getting Ready for BCP
  • Understand BCP approach and how to apply BCP in practice
  • Encourage officials to identify BCP purpose, scope, and team appropriate to their organization
  • Raise awareness of the importance of BCP
2. Conducting Business Impact Analysis
  • Raise awareness of impacts caused by business disruption
  • Identify resumption strategies to recover prioritized activities within Recovery Time Objectives
  • Develop business continuity strategies in 3 phases – before, during, and after incident
3. Performing Risk Assessment
  • Identify, assess, and analyze risks systematically
4. Formulating Business Continuity Strategies
  • Prioritize activities and clarify the components which should be recovered first
  • Identify critical resources needed for prioritized activities including identifying a maximum time that key activities can be unavailable before a disruption becomes unacceptable to the business
  • Raise awareness of the importance of BCP
5. Exercising and updating the BCP
  • Develop BCP based on a PDCA (plan–do–check–act) cycle
  • Understand BCP exercises in different approaches

Success Case :

Transtron (Thailand) Co., Ltd.

The APEC Small and Medium Enterprise Working Group (SMEWG) published the Guidebook on SME Business Continuity planning in 2013.

In 2011, many enterprises in Thailand experienced major disruption to their business operations due to flooding disaster. Transtron (Thailand) Co Ltd was one of the affected enterprises. The company located in Bangkadi Industrial Park (BIP), in Pathum Thani province, was submerged for over a month by water levels as high as 3 meters. However, Transtron was able to continue their business operations at an acceptable level and was able to protect their key machinery and assets during the flood.

After the flood situation, Transtron executives realized the importance of the readiness of the enterprise on business continuity. They recognized that an effective business continuity plan could help lower their losses and ensure the efficient resumption of their operations following disaster events.

Subsequently, Transtron participated in the ‘Resilient SME Champions Program’ organized by ADPC in partnership with DDPM and OSMEP to formulate their plans for minimizing disruption to their business in the face of disaster events.

1

Transtron kept track of the flood situation updates via Bangkadi Industrial Park (BIP) and was able to act on the information received

2

Transtron’s executives realized a situational risk and decided to move their machines and production lines to a designated safe area before the flood was able to inundate their premises

3

Transtron organized a back-up site for administration work and set up an alternative production site at their supplier’s factory in order to continue their operations.
